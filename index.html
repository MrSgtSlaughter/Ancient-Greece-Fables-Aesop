Part I: Aesop’s Fables

Ancient stories. Timeless lessons. ~12 minutes

Background Reading
Who Was Aesop?

Aesop was an ancient Greek storyteller believed to have lived around 620–564 BCE. Though historians debate whether Aesop was a real person, the fables credited to him have been told for over 2,500 years. He was said to have been born into slavery and eventually earned his freedom through his sharp wit and wisdom.

Aesop’s fables are short stories that always feature animals as main characters. These animals speak and behave like humans, and each fable ends with a clear moral — a life lesson. The stories were originally passed down through oral tradition (storytelling by mouth) long before anyone wrote them down.

Some of Aesop’s fables are so famous that we still use phrases from them every day — like “Don’t count your chickens before they hatch” or “Slow and steady wins the race.”

Fable 1 of 3
🐢 The Tortoise and the Hare

A hare was very proud of how fast he could run. One day he mocked a slow tortoise passing by. “You are so slow!” the hare laughed. “You could never beat me in a race!” The tortoise, tired of the hare’s bragging, challenged him. “Fine,” said the hare, smirking. “Let’s race to the old oak tree!”

The race began. The hare darted off so quickly that he was soon far ahead. Thinking he had plenty of time, he lay down in a sunny meadow for a nap. Meanwhile, the tortoise walked slowly but steadily — never stopping, never resting. When the hare finally woke up, he sprinted as fast as he could — but the tortoise was already crossing the finish line.

📚 Moral: “Slow and steady wins the race.”
Fable 2 of 3
🐊 The Boy Who Cried Wolf

A young shepherd boy was bored watching his flock of sheep on a hillside near a village. To amuse himself, he ran toward the village crying, “Wolf! Wolf! The wolf is chasing the sheep!” The villagers came running to help, only to find the boy laughing at them. “There’s no wolf!” he grinned.

He did it again a second time — same result. The villagers shook their heads and went home. Then one afternoon, a real wolf crept from the forest and began attacking the sheep. The boy cried “Wolf! Wolf!” with all his might — but this time, nobody came. They thought he was joking again. The boy had no one to blame but himself.

📚 Moral: “Nobody believes a liar, even when he is telling the truth.”
Fable 3 of 3
🦊 The Fox and the Grapes

One hot summer day, a hungry fox wandered through a vineyard. High on a vine hung a beautiful bunch of ripe, purple grapes. “Those look delicious!” he thought. He jumped and jumped, reaching as high as he could — but the grapes were just out of reach.

After many failed attempts, the exhausted fox finally gave up and walked away. But before he did, he glanced back and said to himself, “Those grapes were probably sour anyway. I didn’t really want them.”

📚 Moral: “It is easy to despise what you cannot have.”

Part II: Homer & The Odyssey

Epic poetry. Epic adventure. ~15 minutes

Background Reading
Who Was Homer?

Homer was an ancient Greek poet believed to have lived around 750 BCE. He is credited with two of the most famous epic poems in all of history: the Iliad (about the Trojan War) and the Odyssey (about the hero’s journey home).

Like Aesop, Homer’s stories began as oral tradition — recited from memory by poets called rhapsodes who performed them at festivals. The poems are enormous: the Odyssey has over 12,000 lines!

The Odyssey follows Odysseus (called Ulysses in Roman mythology), the clever king of Ithaca, as he tries to return home after the 10-year Trojan War. But the journey takes him another 10 years, filled with monsters, gods, and impossible choices. Odysseus is famous not for his strength — but for his cleverness.

Episode 1 of 3 • Book IX
👁 Polyphemus the Cyclops

Odysseus and his men land on a mysterious island and explore a giant cave full of food. Too late, they realize the cave belongs to Polyphemus, a Cyclops (a one-eyed giant) who is the son of the god Poseidon. The Cyclops traps them inside and begins eating two men per meal.

Odysseus must think fast. First, he finds a giant wooden stake, sharpens it to a point, and hides it. Then he offers Polyphemus strong wine until the Cyclops falls into a deep, drunken sleep. While the Cyclops sleeps, Odysseus and his men drive the burning stake into his one eye, blinding him.

When Polyphemus screams in agony, the other Cyclopes outside shout, “Who is hurting you?!” — and Polyphemus can only cry out, “Nobody!” Because Odysseus had told him earlier that his name was “Nobody.” The others assume Polyphemus is fine and leave. In the morning, the blinded Cyclops opens his cave door to let his sheep out — and Odysseus’s men escape by clinging to the undersides of the sheep.

Episode 2 of 3 • Book XII
🎹 The Sirens

The Sirens were dangerous creatures with the bodies of birds and the faces of women. They sat on a rocky island and sang a magical, irresistible song. Any sailor who heard it became so enchanted that he would steer his ship toward the rocks — and crash and die. No one had ever survived hearing the Sirens’ song.

The goddess Circe warned Odysseus about the Sirens and told him exactly how to survive. Odysseus ordered all of his men to plug their ears tightly with beeswax so they could not hear the song at all. But Odysseus himself wanted to hear the legendary music — so he had his men tie him tightly to the ship’s mast.

As the ship passed the island, Odysseus heard the Sirens’ song and was overcome with desperate longing. He screamed and begged his men to untie him so he could swim to them — but his loyal sailors refused, just as he had ordered. They rowed harder until the Sirens were far behind. Odysseus survived — but only because he planned ahead.

Episode 3 of 3 • The Trojan War
🧂 The Trojan Horse

The Trojan War had dragged on for ten years. The Greeks could not break through Troy’s massive stone walls, and the Trojans could not drive the Greeks away. Then Odysseus came up with the most famous trick in history.

The Greeks built a giant wooden horse, large enough to hide a squad of their best soldiers inside. Then the entire Greek army pretended to sail away in defeat, leaving the horse on the beach as if it were an offering to the gods. One Greek soldier, Sinon, stayed behind to convince the Trojans that the horse was a sacred gift — and that bringing it inside the city walls would protect Troy.

The Trojans celebrated their apparent victory and dragged the enormous horse through their gates. That night, while the city slept, the Greek soldiers crept silently out of the horse, opened the city gates for their hidden army, and Troy fell. This plan — born in the mind of Odysseus — ended the longest war in Greek legend. To this day, a “Trojan horse” means any trick that gets inside an enemy’s defenses disguised as something harmless.
